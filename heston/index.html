Methodology

How It Works
01
Why Black-Scholes Is Not Enough

The classic Black-Scholes model assumes volatility is constant — the same value for every strike and every expiry. In real markets this is never true. Options on SPY at different strikes trade at different implied volatilities, forming a curved shape called the volatility smile. Plot this across all strikes and expiries and you get the 3D surface visible above.

Black-Scholes cannot produce this surface — it gives a flat plane. We need a model where volatility is itself random and time-varying.

02
The Heston Model — Stochastic Volatility

Heston (1993) lets variance evolve according to its own stochastic process, coupled to the stock price via correlation ρ:

dS = r·S·dt + √v·S·dW₁
dv = κ(θ − v)·dt + σ·√v·dW₂ , dW₁·dW₂ = ρ·dt

The stock price is driven by noise scaled by √v. Variance v is mean-reverting — pulled toward long-run level θ at speed κ — and itself noisy with vol-of-vol σ. The correlation ρ = −0.95 captures the leverage effect: when SPY drops, volatility spikes. This is why the surface tilts steeply on the left (low strike, high put IV) side.

03
Semi-Analytic Pricing via Fourier Inversion

Heston has no simple closed-form price but does have a known characteristic function — a complete mathematical encoding of the distribution of future stock prices. Using Gil-Pelaez inversion we recover option prices from this function via a one-dimensional integral:

C = S·P₁ − K·e^(−rT)·P₂
Pⱼ = ½ + (1/π) ∫₀^∞ Re[ e^(−iu·log K) · φⱼ(u) / (iu) ] du

P₂ is the risk-neutral probability the stock finishes above K. P₁ is the stock-measure probability (the delta analogue). Both are recovered by integrating the characteristic function φ, evaluated using adaptive numerical quadrature. This is what makes Heston "semi-analytic" — not a simulation, but a fast numerical integral.

04
Calibration — Fitting to 1,542 Market Contracts

We pull the live SPY options chain, extract an implied volatility from each contract's market price via Newton-Raphson, then find the five Heston parameters that minimize the weighted distance between model IVs and market IVs:

L(v₀,κ,θ,σ,ρ) = Σᵢ wᵢ · (IV_model(Kᵢ,Tᵢ) − IV_market(Kᵢ,Tᵢ))²

Weights wᵢ are Gaussian-ATM — near-the-money contracts carry more weight because they are most liquid and carry the most information. This is non-convex, so we use two stages: Differential Evolution (a global population-based search over 1,542 contracts) finds the right basin, then Nelder-Mead refines to convergence.

05
Greeks — Risk Sensitivities via Finite Differences

Once calibrated, the model prices any option. The Greeks measure how sensitive that price is to each input — they are partial derivatives. Heston has no simple analytic Greeks, so we use central finite differences: bump each input symmetrically by a small h and take the ratio:

∂P/∂x ≈ [ P(x+h) − P(x−h) ] / (2h) accuracy: O(h²)

Delta (∂P/∂S) is how much the price changes per $1 move in SPY. Gamma (∂²P/∂S²) is how fast delta changes — critical for dynamic hedging. Vega (∂P/∂σ) is exposure to vol moves. Theta (∂P/∂T) is daily time decay. Together they form the complete risk profile of any position under this model.
